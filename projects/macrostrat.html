Macrostratigraphic constraints on biogeochemical cycles

For my post-doctoral work, I have focused on studying Earth history from a 'Big Data' perspective. Starting in fall of 2014, I have been working with Shanan Peters and Macrostrat, a database that seeks to describe the spatio-temporal distribution of rocks in the upper crust (primarily in North America, but we are expanding!) Macrostrat serves as a platform for organizing and analyzing geological data of all types, and with it, continental scale patterns of sedimentation can be comprehensively quantified (figure right focuses on Paleozoic carbonate distribution). Possibilities for studying biogeochemical cycles of all kinds with Macrostrat are manifold, as exchange with the lithosphere is important for every geochemical process over geological timescales. One hypothesis I am currently testing with Macrostrat is assessing the importance of authigenic carbonate burial. Carbon isotopic measurements (δ¹³C) of marine carbonates are used as proxies for understanding the ancient carbon cycle. When high fidelity δ¹³C time-series can be constructed, and isotopic changes in contemporaneous dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) are inferred, these records typically are interpreted as reflecting the relative importance of two canonical modes of carbon burial: (1) organic matter and (2) marine carbonate. In this framework, temporal changes in δ¹³C result from changes in the fraction of total carbon burial as reduced carbon, which is linked directly to the redox state of Earth’s surface. Recent work, however, has challenged this paradigm, by suggesting that authigenic carbonate, precipitated during early diagenesis, may be an important 'third sink' for carbon that is itself sensitive to changes in atmospheric oxygen concentrations. If this model is correct, our understanding of the carbon cycle and global redox budgets needs to be redressed at a fundamental level. Testing this model is, however, challenging, as authigenic carbonate can be disseminated over a large volume of sediment. Using Macrostrat, we seek to constrain the potential magnitude of the authigenic carbonate burial effect by modeling the three sinks. Because sedimentary lithologies vary in their potential for burying the three phases of carbon, our analysis focuses on quantifying the North American burial flux of individual sedimentary lithotypes. Across a range of reasonable parameters for weight percent organic and authigenic carbonate and their δ¹³C offsets from seawater, the predicted δ¹³C of DIC captures long-term trends seen in composite δ¹³C time-series developed from marine carbonates for the past 900 Myr. Incorporating geochemical measurements into Macrostrat, aided by the machine-reading and -learning initiative GeoDeepDive, will further improve the accuracy and precision of model predictions.
